01 Opening

From what’s happening in the Middle East today,
we learn the same lesson again.

Some countries always believe
starting a war is easier than ending a meeting.

Pause.

In 1961,
the United States thought the same thing.

They planned an invasion
that was supposed to go unnoticed.

They believed their opponent would collapse quickly.

They even thought
this wouldn’t turn into a real war.

Pause.

It took them less than 48 hours
to prove how wrong they were.

This operation was called
the Bay of Pigs Invasion.

02 Why Cuba?

This story starts with one man.

Fidel Castro.

After coming to power,
he did two things.

First, he pushed out American businesses.
Second, he moved closer to the Soviet Union.

The U.S. reaction was simple.

They didn’t like it.
Not even a little.

Because Cuba was too close.

Close enough that you could be drinking coffee in Florida,
while just across the water,
a socialist government was taking shape.

So the question became:

Can we replace him?

03 The CIA’s “Perfect Plan”

The answer was:
maybe.

So the Central Intelligence Agency stepped in.

They came up with a plan
that looked smart on paper.

No direct U.S. military involvement.

Instead, they trained Cuban exiles.

The idea was simple:

Make it look like a local uprising.

Sounds familiar.

The kind of move that says:
“It’s not me… but you know it’s me.”

The core assumption was very confident:

Once they landed,
the Cuban people would support them.

The military would hesitate.

Castro would fall.

In other words,

They weren’t really planning a war.

They were waiting
for a miracle to happen on its own.

04 First Miscalculation

The problem is,

That “miracle”
only existed in their heads.

They seriously overestimated one thing:

How much Cubans hated Castro.

Reality was different.

Most people weren’t ready
to rise up with a group of strangers.

Especially when those strangers
were trained by the United States.

Which made the whole thing… awkward.

You’re trying to start a “local uprising.”

But all your actors
are outsourced.

05 Second Miscalculation

Then came a bigger problem.

They thought
this could be done quietly.

But think about it.

You land troops in another country.
With weapons.
With aircraft.

There’s nothing quiet about that.

And the result was obvious.

The whole world noticed.

This wasn’t an uprising.

It was an invasion.

06 The Operation Begins

April, 1961.

The operation begins.

The trained forces land
on the southern coast of Cuba.

At first,
everything seems fine.

But only for a moment.

Because reality showed up.

Castro’s forces didn’t collapse.

They didn’t hesitate.

They fought back.

Immediately.

07 The Collapse

Then everything started stacking up.

Air support? Not enough.
Supplies? Unstable.
Exit plan? Doesn’t exist.

What does that sound like?

A plan that looks great on paper.

But no one really thought about
what happens if it fails.

And then it got worse.

The U.S. started hesitating.

Because stepping in fully
would mean admitting one thing:

This was their operation.

So they did what governments often do.

They tried to help.

But not all the way.

08 48 Hours Later

The outcome?

Not much suspense.

In less than two days,

The force was surrounded.
Defeated.
Captured.

The operation completely failed.

No uprising.
No miracle.
No comeback.

Just one result:

A very public failure.

09 The Most Embarrassing Part

The worst part wasn’t losing.

It was this:

Something meant to stay secret
became global news.

And it still failed.

It’s like cheating on a test,
getting caught,
and still scoring zero.

10 The Consequences

But the real problem came after.

This failure made Castro stronger.

And it pushed him even closer
to the Soviet Union.

One year later,

The world nearly walked into nuclear war.

The Cuban Missile Crisis.

11 The Pattern

So this story isn’t just about failure.

It’s about a very stable human pattern:

Overestimate yourself.
Underestimate your opponent.
Believe everything will go according to plan.

And then watch it collapse
when reality shows up.

12 Ending

Some countries don’t lose to their enemies.

They lose to their own judgment first.

It’s not that they don’t see the risks.

They just believe—

This time will be different.

Pause.

History doesn’t lack warnings.

It’s just that someone always believes—

This time,
it won’t go wrong.
